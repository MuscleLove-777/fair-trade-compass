Fairtrade Raises Cocoa Minimum Price from October 2026

April 23, 2026 · Category: News & Policy

Starting October 1, 2026, Fairtrade International is raising the minimum price it guarantees cocoa farmers. Ghanaian conventional cocoa jumps to USD 3,500 per metric ton. Ivorian cocoa moves to EUR 3,200. The Fairtrade Premium — the community development payment that sits on top of the minimum price — climbs to USD 275 per ton across all origins. It is one of the steepest revisions the system has seen.

Why the raise, and why now

West African cocoa has been through a brutal two years. Weather disruption and the spread of cocoa swollen shoot virus (CSSVD) slashed yields, and futures markets in London and New York hit historic highs. Paradoxically, that rarely translates into farmer income: smallholders are far from the futures curve, and middlemen absorb most of the upside. A higher minimum price is the mechanism that bridges that gap.

The math behind the headline

Ghana's previous minimum sat at USD 2,400 per ton. The jump to USD 3,500 is roughly a 46% increase. A smallholder household producing a few tons of dry bean per year sees a meaningful lift in real take-home — not a transformation, but a cushion that makes schooling, healthcare, and reinvestment possible in a way thin margins never allowed.

Where the Premium goes

The Premium is paid to the cooperative, not the individual farmer, and members vote on how it is used. Historical deployment has clustered around schools, potable water, women's microfinance, and nursery programs that supply young trees to replace diseased stock. At USD 275 per ton, the Premium pot grows roughly 30% versus current levels, expanding what cooperatives can actually fund.

What it means for chocolate makers

Don't expect the price of a chocolate bar to move materially. Each bar contains only tens of grams of cocoa, so even a USD 1,100 per ton hike translates into pennies at retail. The real pressure is on smaller chocolate makers already operating on tight margins — some will absorb, some will pass through, some may deprioritize Fairtrade sourcing. Consolidation among ethical chocolate brands is a real possibility.

The signal to watch

If you want to read how seriously brands take fair trade, watch the shelves in October. Who maintains pricing? Who quietly drops the Fairtrade mark from a line? Who doubles down and expands certified SKUs? That is where the rhetoric meets the P&L.

Minimum prices matter most precisely when markets are volatile. A floor only works if it is actually held.

Bottom line

A 46% uplift in the cocoa minimum price is a meaningful structural adjustment. For consumers, the cost impact is trivial. For farmers, the cushion is real. For brands, October 2026 is the commitment test.

Sources: Fairtrade International press release, ICCO market reports, Daily Coffee News. Prices as published.
