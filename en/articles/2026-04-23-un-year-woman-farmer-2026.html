2026 UN Year of the Woman Farmer: What It Means for Fair Trade

April 23, 2026 · Category: Producer Stories

The United Nations has declared 2026 the International Year of the Woman Farmer. It is a full year of attention focused on women who grow much of the world's food while being systematically excluded from land ownership, credit, and decision-making. For the fair trade movement, it is also an overdue moment to sharpen its gender programs and show — not just say — that ethical supply chains are gender-aware.

The gender yield gap

According to the UN's Food and Agriculture Organization, women make up roughly 43% of the agricultural labor force in developing countries. Yet fewer than one in five hold land title in their own name. Access to improved seeds, fertilizer, and credit lags far behind that of male farmers. The result: women farmers produce 20-30% less per hectare than men farming the same crop on the same kind of land. Closing that gender yield gap could reduce the number of hungry people in the world by more than 100 million.

What Fairtrade has been doing

Since 2020, Fairtrade International has operated under a dedicated Gender Strategy. Flagship programs include women's leadership schools in East Africa, ring-fenced Premium investments reserved for women-led projects, and community-level interventions that target household decision-making power (which, it turns out, is a harder and more important intervention than any training alone). Colombian coffee cooperatives have led the way by allocating a fixed share of Premium to women-run projects — childcare, rural health posts, savings groups.

New commitments in 2026

Under the 2026-2028 strategy, we expect to see measurable targets: doubling the share of women registered as formal farm operators within three years, and new KPIs that make women's income visible rather than aggregated into household totals. WFTO (the World Fair Trade Organization) is separately moving to strengthen gender-equity audits across its member enterprises.

What consumers can actually do

The clearest consumer lever is product selection. Look for "Women Grown," "Women Led," or Café Femenino-style labels, which confirm that women had a decisive role in production. Coffee, cocoa, and handicrafts from women's cooperatives are more widely available in specialty retail than they were five years ago. Even the simple act of reading the back of the bag trains shoppers to notice when a product has a gender story — and when it deliberately doesn't.

Women farmers are not a minority story. They are a central one. The International Year is a chance to stop treating them as a footnote.

Bottom line

2026 is the year the world is asked to see its food system through women's eyes. Policy, data, and product decisions will all be interrogated against that lens. Fair Trade Compass will follow women producers throughout the year — in their own words, wherever possible.

Sources: FAO gender reports, Fairtrade International Gender Strategy, WFTO annual reports. Written from publicly available information.
