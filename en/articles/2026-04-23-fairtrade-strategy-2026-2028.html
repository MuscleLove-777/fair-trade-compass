Fairtrade's 2026-2028 Strategy Puts Farmer Resilience First

April 23, 2026 · Category: News & Policy

Fairtrade International has announced its new three-year strategy for 2026 through 2028. If you read only the press release, one phrase keeps coming back: farmer resilience. The certification body is repositioning itself around the idea that in a world of climate volatility, price swings, and tightening regulation, the most valuable thing it can offer smallholders is the ability to absorb shocks.

Three pillars of the new strategy

The architecture is refreshingly simple. First, strengthen price protection for flagship commodities — coffee, cocoa, tea, bananas, spices. Second, invest in producer networks and cooperatives so farmers are organized, represented, and able to negotiate. Third, build the traceability infrastructure that will let producers comply with a wave of new due-diligence laws, starting with the EU's Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive (CSDDD).

"Evolved standards" land in 2027

The highest-impact piece is a full standards overhaul. A public consultation runs through 2026 involving the three regional producer networks, twenty-five national marketing organizations, and Flocert (Fairtrade's independent certifier). The updated "evolved standards" will be published in 2027, with certification under the new regime expected to begin by 2028. Existing certified operators will receive a transition window, but first movers are already being rewarded in commercial negotiations.

What it means for coffee

Coffee is Fairtrade's single biggest product category by volume, and it sits at the sharp end of climate change. The new strategy pairs price protection with direct investment in climate adaptation: shade-tree programs, drought-resistant varieties, diversification into non-coffee income. Expect to see more cooperatives channeling Fairtrade Premium funds into resilience infrastructure rather than social projects alone.

Fruits and vegetables, now unified

Another quiet but meaningful change: the separate standards for Fresh Fruits, Fresh Vegetables, and Prepared and Preserved Fruits and Vegetables will be merged into a single Fruits and Vegetables Standard. For buyers sourcing bananas, pineapples, avocados, and mangoes, compliance will get cleaner. For traders sitting on legacy contracts, there will be some paperwork.

Why buyers should care

For retailers and manufacturers, 2026 is effectively a blueprint year. The brands that engage with the consultation, map their supply chains against the evolved standards, and begin pilot compliance work will be in a much stronger commercial position by 2028 than those who wait. In Japan alone, more than 240 companies already carry Fairtrade certification. The pool will grow, but so will the differentiation between leaders and laggards.

The shift is from "protect farmers from shocks" to "equip farmers to face shocks on their own terms." Certification is no longer the endpoint — it's the launchpad.

Bottom line

The 2026-2028 strategy is the biggest structural refresh Fairtrade has undertaken in a decade. If you're a consumer, watch which brands move early. If you're a buyer, this is the year to engage with the consultation rather than observe it from the sidelines.
